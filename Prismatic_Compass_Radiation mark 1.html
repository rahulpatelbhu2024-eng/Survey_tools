Prismatic Compass — Core Theory

What is a Prismatic Compass?

The most convenient and portable form of magnetic compass — can be hand-held or fitted on a tripod. It uses Earth's magnetic field to give directly magnetic bearings of lines. A prism and sighting slit define the line of sight; the prism enables simultaneous viewing of the graduated compass card and the distant object.

💡 Daily Life Analogy
Prismatic Compass = Google Maps compass on steroids

Your phone compass tells you which direction you're facing — but you can only read it separately from what you're looking at. The prismatic compass is like a phone that shows your facing direction and the target object in the same eyepiece simultaneously, like the AR navigation arrows in Google Maps overlaid on the camera view.

PrismThe AR overlay in your phone camera — bends the reading to your eye while you sight the target.
Magnetic needleGPS signal — always points to true reference regardless of where you walk.
Graduated ringThe compass rose on Google Maps — 0° to 360° painted on the ring.

Radiation Method — How it works

  • Set up compass at ONE fixed base station O
  • Sight each boundary/object point; read its WCB bearing
  • Measure distance O→point with chain/tape
  • In office: draw N-line at O, plot each ray at its bearing, mark distance to scale
  • Join ray tips in order → survey map
  • Suitable for small, open, levelled areas
💡 Daily Life Analogy
Radiation = Lighthouse beam sweeping the harbour

A lighthouse at one fixed point (O) sweeps its beam to every ship in the harbour. Each ship is like your object point — the angle of the beam when it hits the ship is the bearing, and the distance is how far the light travels. Your survey map is the harbour chart drawn from the lighthouse's perspective.

Base station OThe lighthouse — one fixed position controls everything.
BearingThe angle of the beam when it illuminates each ship.
DistanceThe length of the light beam to each ship.
RayThe actual beam of light drawn on the chart.

Bearing Systems — WCB vs QB

▼

WCB (Whole Circle Bearing)

Angle measured clockwise from North, 0° to 360°. Used in prismatic compass. Example: S45°W = 225° WCB.

QB (Quadrantal Bearing)

Angle from N or S towards E or W, 0°–90°. Used in surveyor's compass. Example: N60°E, S30°W. Must convert to WCB for calculations.

Field Procedure (Step by Step)

▼
  1. Make a reconnaissance and select base station O in the centre of the area
  2. Centre compass over O using plumb bob or string
  3. Level by adjusting tripod legs so needle swings freely
  4. Focus prism by moving up/down on focusing stud
  5. Release needle only when compass is approximately in magnetic meridian
  6. Sight object A — align object vane, hair, and eye vane
  7. Read bearing from prism (avoid parallax — look along needle, not across)
  8. Measure distance OA with chain/tape
  9. Tap compass gently before reading to overcome pivot friction
  10. Record bearing and distance in field notebook
  11. Repeat for all object points B, C, D, E… in sequence
  12. Take duplicate readings at important stations

💡 Daily Life Analogy
Three methods = three ways to map your BHU hostel corridor

Radiation: stand at the corridor junction (O) and measure distance + direction to every room door. Triangulation: stand at two ends and note which direction each door is from both ends — intersection gives position. Traverse: walk from Room 1 to Room 2 to Room 3, recording each step's direction and length.
